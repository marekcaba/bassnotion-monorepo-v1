# The Bassicology Funnel — Three Doors, One Playable Instrument

> _"They describe practice. We **are** practice."_

This is the whole acquisition vision in one place. Read it top to bottom and you'll understand not just **what** we're building, but **why** every piece is shaped the way it is. The funnel isn't a stack of assets bolted together. It's a single idea — _practice, don't watch_ — expressed at every layer, all the way from a stranger scrolling a feed to a player who lives in the platform forever.

---

## The one belief everything hangs on

Bass education has spent fifteen years confusing **watching** with **learning**. Everyone makes videos _about_ grooves. Lectures. Demos. "Here's how it goes, subscribe."

Bassicology is built the other way around: **almost nothing to watch, everything to do.**

That belief isn't just our product philosophy — it's our funnel philosophy. The funnel itself has to obey the rule. If Bassicology is the thing you _do_ instead of _watch_, then the funnel can't be a thing people watch their way through. It has to be a thing they **play** their way through. The free experience puts the bass in their hands _before_ any pitch ever shows up.

Every layer below does the same core thing: **the player plays, the platform structures.** That's the thread. Hold it the whole way down.

---

## The shape: brain speed slows as trust deepens

The funnel has a spine, and the spine is **brain speed**. People consume short-form at ~90 mph, YouTube at ~50, email at ~20. Trust deepens as the speed slows. So we never promote the slow thing on the fast platform — and the offers that need the most trust live at the slowest layers.

```
Shorts (IG + YT, 90 mph)   →  win 8 bars, make them grab the bass
        ↓
Weekly YouTube (50 mph)    →  one groove, deconstructed live, played WITH them
        ↓
Three doors → the platform →  the AHA, then the assessment, then the account
        ↓
Email sequence (20 mph)    →  a daily rep in the inbox, then the offer escalates
        ↓
The platform               →  playable free; depth, tools, programs paid
```

## The big idea: THREE DOORS into the platform

Here's the thing most funnels get wrong — they build **one** front door and force everyone through it at the same speed. But our traffic arrives at different **temperatures** and from different **sources**, and a single door can't serve them.

**A door is sized to the temperature of the person walking through it — and earns its place only if it catches a source the other doors can't.**

The three doors: the **landing page** (cold ad/search clickers), the **weekly YouTube video** (warm viewers), and the **pain-point SEO/GEO blog** (high-intent problem-searchers). All three funnel to the same assessment, the same platform, the same philosophy — no overlapping effort.

### Door 1 — The Landing Page (COLD traffic)

The person clicked an ad, a short, a search result. They've seen nothing. Zero trust, zero orientation. Thirty seconds ago they didn't know we existed.

A cold person won't sign up before they feel something. So the **AHA has to happen first**, right on the page, with **zero commitment**.

### Door 2 — The Weekly YouTube Video (WARM traffic)

The person just watched us drive the platform as an instrument for ten minutes. They've seen the full uncapped version work. They understand the interface. They feel the device. They're pre-sold and pre-oriented before they ever click.

A warm person doesn't need a taste — the whole video _was_ the taste. So we deliver the exact groove they watched, directly, the moment they click.

### Door 3 — The Pain-Point SEO/GEO Blog (HIGH-INTENT SEARCH traffic)

Someone types "why does my bass sound muddy" into Google — or asks ChatGPT/Perplexity. They have the highest intent of anyone in the funnel: not browsing, actively trying to fix a specific problem right now. Neither other door catches them.

The post answers the question — but the fix is a **playable widget**, not a paragraph. That's the one thing the AI-overview era can't strip, because the value isn't text Google can lift; it's an experience that only exists on the page. The post _is_ a problem-specific landing page that catches them mid-problem and hands them the assessment.

**Both the cold and warm doors run the same trick, wearing two outfits: never _describe_ the platform — _be_ the platform.** On YouTube, we drive the instrument for them. On the landing page, they drive a tiny slice of it themselves. Same wow, two angles — like watching a pro demo a guitar in a shop versus picking it up yourself.

> **Doors point forward into the platform — never sideways into each other.** The only thing that travels between doors is _content_ (the blog hosting the YouTube video to help it rank), never _traffic_ (a video's warm viewers must never be sent backward to a slower blog). Each door serves its native traffic at its native speed.

**On the SEO/GEO door specifically:** don't chase informational "what is X" queries — AI Overviews answer those and the click dies. Chase queries whose answer is a _doing_ ("why does MY bass sound muddy"), because the playable-widget answer is the one thing the zero-click era can't strip. And optimize to be _cited and recommended by the AI engines_ (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini), not just ranked by Google — those referrals convert far better. The playbook in brief: let the AI crawlers in (robots.txt for GPTBot, ClaudeBot, PerplexityBot, etc.; get into Bing, since ChatGPT starts there), write extractably (lead with a 40–60 word answer, specific dated claims, FAQ/table structure), and build off-site trust (Reddit, YouTube, third-party mentions). Measure weekly; validate on Perplexity first (2–4 week feedback). _Treat GEO tactics as fast-moving — re-verify specifics before investing heavily._

## DOOR 2 IN DETAIL — The Weekly YouTube Video

This is the warm door, and it's the heart of the content engine.

### The format: the platform IS the instrument

We don't make videos _about_ the platform. We make videos where the platform **is the instrument we play.** On screen, live: set up the groove, slow it down, speed it up, pitch it up, switch to drummer-only, then click-only, mute the bass and take the seat.

The viewer isn't watching a feature tour. They're watching the instrument _be useful_ — which is the only kind of demo that ever converts.

### Why this resolves the "don't watch" question

A fair objection: _if the video's purpose is for people to watch it, doesn't that break "don't watch, practice"?_

No — and untangling it sharpens the whole brand. **The rule was never "don't watch video." It's "don't let watching replace the reps."** The enemy is _substitution_, not the medium.

A 10-minute video breathes between **two modes**:

- **Reveal + explanation** — this part _is_ watching, and that's fine. It teaches the device and creates the desire to practice.
- **Play-along** — "grab your bass, we're playing this together, four counts in." Bass in hand, real reps, you as the bandmate. _This part is practice, on camera._

The video doesn't break the rule. It **performs** it — the watching earns the doing. We'd be the only bass channel people _play along to_ instead of just watch. That's a moat competitors can't clone, because the format requires our software.

### The built-in wall

The video play-along is **capped by the medium itself** — for free. Video can only run at one tempo (ours, not theirs), one key, one pass. It can't loop the bar they keep flubbing. It can't mute our bass so they truly take the seat. It can't score them.

Those limits _are_ the pitch: _"That's the taste. To slow it to your tempo, mute me, loop the hard bar, and own it — the full version's in the link below."_

### The handoff

Because the whole video was the platform, the CTA has **zero seam**. "Keep practicing with these controls — link below" isn't a gear-shift into a pitch; it's "go use the thing you just watched work."

They click → create an account → **the exact groove they watched is waiting**, already cued up. No "what is this, where do I start" — the biggest drop-off in every creator platform — because they already know the interface and already want this specific groove.

### Bonus payoffs

- **Un-clonable content.** Anyone can talk over a webcam. Nobody else can show _our_ platform doing tempo/key/mute/isolate, because they don't have it. The moat is visible in every video.
- **Free demo footage.** Every weekly video is also B-roll for the landing page, ads, and email. One shoot, many assets.
- **The platform looks alive.** Seeing the creator practice in their own tool every week signals "this is where the real work happens" — the trust the high-ticket tiers need.

> **The one discipline:** _play_ the interface, never _operate_ it. Every control you touch must be because the groove needs it right now — never a tutorial of the UI. Set up off-camera; on camera, the controls move in service of the music.

## DOOR 1 IN DETAIL — The Landing Page

This is the cold door, and it has to manufacture the AHA the way the video does for warm traffic.

### Two ways to build it (and which to start with)

**Option A — Playable taste on the page.** The hero of the page is a live, playable slice of the platform: press play, drag the tempo down, mute the bass, feel yourself take the seat — in 60–90 seconds, no account. The landing-page equivalent of the video, except _they_ drive it.

**Option B — Fast static page → the assessment is the AHA.** The page is bone-fast, static, one job: sell one click into the free assessment. The assessment itself (where they play 5 short exercises and get scored) becomes the hands-on AHA.

### The page-speed reality

Loading the **whole engine** on the landing page is a no-go — slow load, wrecked Core Web Vitals, tanked Google ranking. But that's a false choice: **the AHA needs a doorbell, not the cathedral.**

The AHA only needs one groove and two working controls — a few small audio files and ~50 lines of JS. If we keep a playable taste (Option A), it's engineered light:

- **Static page renders instantly** (text, one image, the control shell) — that's what Google ranks.
- **Audio + widget JS load lazily** — on hover or first tap of play. Zero cost until they want it.
- **"Slow it" / "mute bass" swap pre-rendered audio clips** — no live engine running on the page.

> **Recommendation:** Given the strong page-speed/SEO priority, **start with Option B** — fast static page selling the click into the assessment, where the playing and AHA happen. Then A/B test adding a lightweight taste only if conversion data demands it. Don't add weight you haven't proven you need.

## WHERE THE FREE ASSESSMENT LIVES

The assessment is a small commitment, and the rule for commitments is simple:

> **The assessment goes right after the AHA — never before it.**

That single rule places it differently in each door, because the doors deliver the AHA at different points.

|                         | **Landing page (cold)**                    | **YouTube (warm)**                                        |
| ----------------------- | ------------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------- |
| **The AHA**             | Happens on the page / in the assessment    | Already happened — the 10-min video                       |
| **Assessment position** | **Before** the account                     | **After** the account                                     |
| **Assessment's job**    | **Converts** — it's what earns the email   | **Personalizes** — it deepens an already-converted player |
| **Sequence**            | Taste/AHA → assessment → account → grooves | Video → account → capped groove → assessment → grooves    |

**Same assessment, two jobs.** On cold traffic it's the conversion mechanism. On warm traffic it's the personalization-and-deepening mechanism.

### What the assessment must be

It must **be playing, not self-reporting.** Per the blueprint: 5 short exercises, ~3 minutes, the Bridge measures actual ability across domains. Never "rate yourself 1–5 on syncopation." The assessment itself has to _be_ the AHA, not the homework before it. Keep it short, keep it fun. The moment it becomes a quiz, we've put homework before the dopamine.

### The account gate

The gate is framed as **saving their result**, never as a paywall: _"Create an account to keep your assessment and unlock the 3 grooves we picked for you."_ The account is where their progress _lives_ — not a wall on content they already earned. One tap. Email + password or one-click auth. Every extra field leaks intent at the highest-intent moment in the entire funnel.

## THE PLATFORM IS PLAYABLE — the free/paid constitution

The single load-bearing principle, the one that resolves every "free or paid?" question forever:

> **The platform is fully _playable_ on a free account. Access is free. Depth, tools, and programs are paid.**

A free account gets a genuinely playable instrument — press play, hear the groove and drummer, mute the bass, take the seat, get the AHA, collect and conquer grooves. It is not a demo or a locked screen. It really plays. What it _doesn't_ give is unlimited depth, the full tool suite, or structured programs — and that's the line.

**The line is _taste vs unlimited_, never _practice vs no practice_.** Putting the ability to practice behind the paywall would hide the AHA — the exact moment that converts a cold visitor — so the funnel would stop converting. The free user practices for real; they just keep hitting the ceiling, and paying removes it.

### The permanent gating rule

Every future feature gets sorted by one question:

> **If it's part of _playing a groove_ → free (capped). If it's _depth on_, _a tool beside_, or _a program around_ playing → paid.**

- Playing a groove — play, mute, take the seat, one pull of each lever → **free** (that's "playable")
- Unlimited depth — full tempo range, all keys, infinite loop, scoring/progress → **paid**
- Tools beside playing — widgets (ear training, sight reading, groove trainer), notation → **paid** (a single free taste of one widget is fine as a hook)
- Programs around playing — technique packs, accelerator, future reading/genre programs → **paid** (the storefront)

### The free-groove wall — taste, never the whole pie

A **fully unlocked** groove is a complete practice tool forever — they'd never need to buy. So the wall doesn't live _around the catalog_; it lives **inside each lever.**

> **A lever they can pull once is a taste. A lever they can live on is the pie.** Free lets them pull every lever exactly once.

| Lever                     | Free (the taste)               | Paid (the uncap)              |
| ------------------------- | ------------------------------ | ----------------------------- |
| **Mute & jam**            | Mute the bass once, jam 8 bars | Loop infinitely, every groove |
| **Tempo**                 | One slower preset and back     | Full 40–200 BPM dial          |
| **Transpose**             | Shift to one other key         | All 12 keys                   |
| **Deconstruction layers** | See them exist (greyed)        | Isolating & drilling them     |
| **Scoring / Bridge**      | One baseline score             | Progress tracked over time    |

**Every lever really works once** — that's the AHA, and it keeps the brand promise honest. Each cap **teaches the specific upsell**: when they hit the tempo wall, they learn exactly what they're buying. The cap must feel like a **generous teacher** ("here's how much more of this real thing exists"), never a **fake locked button** ("gotcha"). The test: would a player _remark_ on it to another musician? "It actually transposed, and I realized the paid version does all 12 keys" sells itself. "It showed me a fake locked button" breaks trust.

### How the free library is accessed

All free grooves live **openly in the platform** once you have an account — browsable, not re-gated through the videos. But **each groove is paired with its YouTube video, presented as the way to actually learn it.** Don't gate with a wall; gate with _comprehension_ — you _can_ grab a groove alone, but without its video breakdown it's a brick without the blueprint. This keeps collecting frictionless _and_ keeps the video essential, pulling collectors back to YouTube.

Optional framing: split the library into "the back catalog" (open, the collector's playground) and "new this week" (spotlights the current video), so the open library still drives fresh weekly views.

### Let them collect — don't fear it

_Worry:_ a user clicks every free link across many videos and ends up with 60 free grooves.

_Reality:_ **that's the dream, not the danger.** To get 60 grooves they watched ~60 videos and came back week after week — the highest content-minutes in the whole audience, the most pre-sold buyer we'll ever have. And 60 _capped_ grooves is 60 _demos_ — 60 run-ins with the wall. Count is harmless; only **depth** was ever the risk, and depth is already walled.

> Don't prevent the collection — **monetize it.** "You own 60 grooves. Unlock full tempo, all 12 keys, deconstruction, and scoring across _every one_." The bigger their free library, the stronger the membership pitch. The collector is the success case.

## THE GAME — "play 'em all," not "catch 'em all"

The free tier is a collecting game, tuned so the addiction points at practice. The governing rule, because it's easy to get wrong:

> **Reward the rep, never the acquisition.** A grabbed-but-unplayed groove counts for nothing — that's hoarding, the watcher's disease. The "catch" isn't "I unlocked this groove." It's "I _can play_ it."

### Earn the next groove

We don't dump the whole library on them. They unlock the next groove by **demonstrating progress on the current one** — gated on the Bridge's score ("you can play this now"), _not_ on raw repetition count ("you pressed play 4 times"). Gating on a quota builds a treadmill that rewards attendance over learning and demoralizes the stuck-intermediate avatar. Gating on demonstrated ability rewards the only thing that matters.

So the loop: practice current groove → hit a passing score → choose your next → it unlocks → repeat. **The only currency is practice.** Hoarding is structurally impossible because the next groove exists only after you've genuinely played the last.

### Let the player steer (and feed the engine)

At each unlock, they choose direction: **harder · same level · easier**, plus a genre lean. This does two jobs: it gives the player _agency_ (a chosen path completes far better than a marched one), and every tap quietly feeds the adaptive personalization engine their perceived difficulty and taste — the assessment and Closed-Loop Journey System, disguised as a fun choice.

**Crucial:** the "too hard / easier" branch is also the **release valve.** If someone genuinely can't pass a groove, the system must offer a step down rather than trapping them behind the gate feeling like a failure. Vary the unlock criteria too (play it clean / at tempo / in a new key) so the gate stays a fresh challenge, not a repeated toll booth.

### Mastery tiers and sets

A conquered groove isn't binary — it has tiers (bronze → silver → gold as clean-tempo and keys climb), giving players a reason to return to grooves they already beat (the depth that builds real skill, and the thing that kills hoarding — why grab 40 when each conquered one still has gold waiting). And twelve grooves on one device form a _set_ — conquering the set is mastery of the technique, which is literally the paid Pack. The free hunt naturally surfaces the paid sets as the "gym badges" worth chasing.

## THE WEEKLY ENGINE — one rep, four jobs

Once a week we create **one original bass groove** — our own drums, our own keys — and it does four jobs at once: the YouTube video, the free funnel groove, the retention hook, and a brick in the paid catalog.

### Copyright: principle, not notes

We start _inspired by_ a famous line — for the title and the search traffic and the verbal hook. But we **teach the principle behind it, not the notes of it**, and write a genuinely original groove that demonstrates that principle.

> **The test:** would a musicologist say "same line" (you copied) or "same trick, different line" (you're clear)? Aim for the second, always. Principles aren't copyrightable; specific note sequences are.

This isn't a constraint fighting the brand — it _is_ the brand. We teach transferable principles on original grooves. The famous line lives in the title and the spoken analysis; every note we play and ship is ours.

**Two copyrights to respect:** the _composition_ (notes) and the _recording_ (the master track). The recording is the one Content ID auto-claims within minutes — **the original studio recording never goes in the timeline, and never into the platform.** Naming a song and analyzing it in words is fine. Performing your own original groove is the safe, transferable move.

**On the platform side this matters even more than on YouTube.** A claimed video is one demonetized video. But shipping copyrighted audio _inside the product_ converts a clean 95%-margin software business into a music-licensing business — a different, far worse company. Every groove, backing, drum, and key part we ship is original and owned. _(Not legal advice — get a music-rights attorney to bless the "principle not notes" standard before scaling. Cheap insurance on an expensive asset.)_

### The bonus: the weekly rep is also a retention engine

Every week a new groove lands in the free library. The free tier is **never static** — there's always something new waiting. A free user from three months ago has a reason to come back every week. The thing we do for copyright safety _also_ keeps the audience alive — the exact opposite of the Dead Audience Trap.

## THE EMAIL SEQUENCE — a rep in the inbox

Email is our **direct pipe** — the hedge against feeds dying. It's the slowest brain speed, so it's where we go deep and where the real selling happens. But our brand poisons the standard "value email, then pitch email" playbook. **A Bassicology email can't be a thing they read. It has to send them to their bass.**

Every email is a 5-minute rep, delivered: _"Open this. Loop it. Mute the bass. Play just the ghost notes for 5 minutes. Reply and tell me what you noticed."_

**Personalized from email one** — because we captured their assessment scores at signup. Not a generic drip; "your timing scored 64, so here's your first timing rep."

The welcome arc over ~14 days:

- **Days 1–5 — five grooves, five reps.** Pure giving. No selling. The list proves it does what the brand promises.
- **Day 6 — the reframe.** _"You've done 5 reps this week. Most people who follow a bass account did zero."_ Consumer → player. First soft mention that the limits come off with membership.
- **Days 7–8 — the uncap rep.** Show them hitting the ceiling, then → **Membership ($24/mo)**: "practice without limits — the whole library, every tempo and key, plus the tools."
- **Days 9–10 — the technique rep** → **Groove Pack ($39)**: "understand it isn't enough — drill the cheat codes until it's in your fingers."
- **Days 11–12 — the path** → **4-Week Accelerator ($97)**: "the hard part was never the playing — it's the deciding. Open it, press play, finish in a month."
- **At launch only — the founding pitch** → **Founding Membership ($397, first 100, lifetime)**: "get the keys for life — and fund the build." Real scarcity, runs once during the launch window, then closes. Slots in wherever the launch calendar puts it, not as a permanent step.
- **Day 13+ — the engine room.** Non-buyers drop into the weekly rhythm: each new video groove becomes an email rep, and the in-platform game keeps surfacing the gap. Re-pitch on **triggers** (conquered a set, hit the ceiling, a new pack ships), never on a calendar.

**Offer rules baked in:** never present lower tiers as alternatives during a higher-tier moment (sequence them over days, not stacked on one page); the membership is the one obvious recurring yes, packs and accelerator are à la carte boosters on top; the list never dies — it becomes the slow lane the weekly video pours into.

The products aren't competing tiers — each sells a **different verb the free tier structurally can't perform.** That's why a player can be completely happy in the free tier and _still_ have an unmet need. Free saturates one need; paid serves the others. They stack, they don't compete.

### Founding Membership — fund the build (launch only)

A one-time, used-once-then-closed offer to raise launch cash and create evangelists:

> **First 100 members. $397, one payment. Lifetime membership.** "Get the keys for life — and fund the build."

The pricing logic: a lifetime deal must be priced at _several years of the annual rate_, because that's what a committed player gets out of it. At ~$24/mo, $397 is roughly two years upfront for forever — generous to the buyer, real cash for us. Pricing it at one year (~$197) would hand our highest-LTV customers a permanent ~80–90% discount and delete our best 100 subscribers from the recurring line that _is_ the business. The higher price also _signals_ the membership's value; a cheap lifetime makes the product look weak. And the true story — "the first 100 fund the build" — makes founders feel like patrons, not customers, which justifies the price.

**What lifetime covers (the boundary that protects the catalog):**

> **Lifetime = the recurring membership, forever — the room and everything that lands in it. NOT the à la carte products.**

- **Included:** unlimited practice (full tempo, all keys, loop, scoring), the never-capped widgets, notation, and every future _widget and platform improvement_ as it ships — because "everything new in the room" is what membership already promises.
- **Separate, even for founders:** Groove Packs ($39 each) and the 4-Week Accelerator ($97) stay à la carte. They're the expanding catalog we sell repeatedly to the base — folding them into a lifetime deal would exempt our 100 best customers from the entire product line built to monetize them (the same under-pricing mistake, one layer down).
- **Excluded:** 1:1 coaching — scarce human time, never bundled into a software deal, or we'd owe 100 people free coaching forever.

The rule in one line: **lifetime covers the _room_, not the _furniture you keep buying_.**

### Understanding is free; ability is paid

The trap to avoid: the YouTube video _deconstructs_ the groove, and the Pack _sells_ the deconstruction — so doesn't the video give the product away? No, because there are two different things:

- **Understanding** — knowing _how_ the groove works (the ghost notes make it breathe). Cheap to give; it markets you. Give it away on YouTube freely.
- **Ability** — _being able to play it_ clean, at tempo, in any key, in your own playing. Understanding never produces ability — that's our company's entire premise (people watch thousands of lessons and stay stuck).

> The gap between _understanding it_ and _being able to play it_ is the product. No video can close it, because no video can practice for you. The video shows the X-ray; the Pack is the gym that builds the muscle the X-ray revealed.

The discipline: free may _show_ the deconstruction layers (understanding), but must _cap the drilling_ of them (ability). Free reveals the layers; paid lets you practice them into your hands across a sequenced set.

### The coherence test

Each tier must pass one test: _can a happy customer of the tier below still have an unmet need this tier serves?_

- Free user (happy playing) → "I keep hitting the ceiling, I want to really practice this." → **Membership** ✓
- Member (practicing unlimited) → "I understand these but still can't _nail_ the technique." → **Pack** ✓
- Member + packs (drilling techniques) → "I don't know what to do each day, I keep drifting." → **Accelerator** ✓

Each serves a need the one below genuinely still has. Nobody pays twice for the same thing.

### The expanding catalog — the long-game business

The membership is the recurring heart; the packs are an **open-ended product line sold on the platform** — technique packs, speed-reading, sight-reading, genre intensives, future programs. Each is a new thing to sell to the base already inside (far cheaper than acquiring new users), and each makes the room more worth being in. "A practice room that keeps getting better" becomes literal.

> **Keep the decision simple as the catalog grows.** Membership stays the one obvious recurring yes. Organize packs by the _outcome_ the player wants ("read faster," "nail funk," "lock your timing"), so picking is choosing a destination — never browsing a wall of SKUs.

### Three audiences the free tier sorts for us, for free

- **Watchers** — collect, never practice, never buy. _Never lost sales_ — they were never going to buy from anyone. They're our reach and proof, the wide top of the funnel.
- **Doers** — pick up the bass, hit the wall, buy because they want to _do_ the thing. The wall converts them.
- **Collectors** — the 60-groove people. The bigger their library, the stronger the membership pitch.
