# Machiavelli's Key Teachings - Knowledge Base

This document contains the essential teachings and wisdom from Machiavelli's works, organized by theme for quick reference during conversations.

## On Acquiring Power

### Types of Principalities
- **Hereditary**: Easier to maintain - just don't transgress ancestral customs
- **New**: Harder to hold - you must establish new foundations
- **Mixed/Composite**: Parts annexed to existing state - requires careful management
- **Ecclesiastical**: Sustained by religious tradition - hardest to acquire, easiest to keep

### Methods of Acquisition
- By one's own arms and ability (virtù) - most stable
- By fortune and others' arms - least stable
- By wickedness - can gain power but not glory
- By citizen support - either from nobles or people

### Key Principle
> "Those who solely by good fortune become princes have little trouble in rising, but much in keeping atop; they have not any difficulties on the way up, because they fly, but they have many when they reach the summit."

## On Maintaining Power

### The Foundation
> "The chief foundations of all states are good laws and good arms; and as there cannot be good laws where the state is not well armed, it follows that where they are well armed they have good laws."

### Essential Strategies
1. **Reside in new territories** - See problems as they arise, not when too late
2. **Send colonies** - Cheaper than garrisons, offends only a few
3. **Weaken the powerful, protect the weak** - Balance of power
4. **Never allow foreign powers to gain footing** - Prevention over cure
5. **Deal with problems early** - "In the beginning of the malady it is easy to cure but difficult to detect"

### On Conquered Free Cities
Three options for cities accustomed to liberty:
1. Ruin them
2. Reside there in person
3. Let them govern themselves under tribute with loyal oligarchy

> "He who becomes master of a city accustomed to freedom and does not destroy it, may expect to be destroyed by it."

## On Military Matters

### Types of Forces
- **Own forces**: Best - composed of subjects, citizens, dependants
- **Mercenaries**: Dangerous - disunited, unfaithful, cowardly in real danger
- **Auxiliaries**: Even worse - united but under another's control
- **Mixed**: Better than pure mercenary/auxiliary, worse than own

### The Prince's Primary Study
> "A prince ought to have no other aim or thought, nor select anything else for his study, than war and its rules and discipline; for this is the sole art that belongs to him who rules."

### Practical Training
- Hunt to learn terrain
- Study geography of one's country
- Read histories of great commanders
- Always be mentally at war, even in peace

## On The Nature of Princes

### The Reality vs. The Ideal
> "It being my intention to write a thing which shall be useful to him who apprehends it, it appears to me more appropriate to follow up the real truth of a matter than the imagination of it; for many have pictured republics and principalities which in fact have never been known or seen."

### Qualities to APPEAR to Have
- Merciful
- Faithful
- Humane
- Religious
- Upright

### Actual Behavior
> "Therefore it is necessary for a prince to know how to do wrong, and to make use of it or not according to necessity."

## On Fear vs. Love

### The Core Teaching
> "Upon this a question arises: whether it be better to be loved than feared or feared than loved? It may be answered that one should wish to be both, but, because it is difficult to unite them in one person, it is much safer to be feared than loved."

### Why Fear is More Reliable
> "Men have less scruple in offending one who is beloved than one who is feared, for love is preserved by the link of obligation which, owing to the baseness of men, is broken at every opportunity for their advantage; but fear preserves you by a dread of punishment which never fails."

### The Balance
- Be feared but NOT hated
- Never touch property or women of subjects
- When executing, have proper justification
- Men forget father's death before loss of patrimony

## On Cruelty Well-Used

### Proper vs. Improper Cruelty
> "Those may be called properly used, if of evil it is lawful to speak well, that are applied at one blow and are necessary to one's security, and that are not persisted in afterwards."

### The Key Principle
> "Injuries ought to be done all at one time, so that, being tasted less, they offend less; benefits ought to be given little by little, so that the flavour of them may last longer."

### Example: Cesare Borgia
Used Ramiro d'Orco to pacify the Romagna with cruelty, then publicly executed him to distance himself from the harshness and win the people's favor.

## On Faith-Keeping

### The Lion and the Fox
> "A prince, therefore, being compelled knowingly to adopt the beast, ought to choose the fox and the lion; because the lion cannot defend himself against snares and the fox cannot defend himself against wolves."

### When to Break Faith
> "A wise lord cannot, nor ought he to, keep faith when such observance may be turned against him, and when the reasons that caused him to pledge it exist no longer."

### The Art of Seeming
> "It is unnecessary for a prince to have all the good qualities I have enumerated, but it is very necessary to appear to have them."

## On Avoiding Hatred and Contempt

### What Causes Hatred
- Being rapacious
- Violating property of subjects
- Violating women of subjects

### What Causes Contempt
- Being considered fickle
- Being frivolous
- Being effeminate
- Being mean-spirited
- Being irresolute

### The Defense Against Conspiracy
> "One of the most efficacious remedies that a prince can have against conspiracies is not to be hated and despised by the people."

## On Counsel and Advisors

### The Three Intellects
1. Those who comprehend by themselves - excellent
2. Those who appreciate what others comprehend - good
3. Those who neither comprehend nor learn - useless

### Managing Advisors
> "A prince ought always to take counsel, but only when he wishes and not when others wish."

### Avoiding Flatterers
- Choose wise men who may speak truth
- Let them know frank speech is valued
- Question them on everything
- Form own conclusions
- Be steadfast in resolutions

## On Fortune

### The Role of Fortune
> "I believe that fortune controls half our actions, but we can resist her through preparation and boldness."

### Preparing for Fortune
> "A wise prince ought to observe some such rules, and never in peaceful times stand idle, but increase his resources with industry in such a way that they may be available to him in adversity, so that if fortune changes it may find him prepared to resist her blows."

### Fortune Favors the Bold
Fortune is like a woman - she favors the impetuous, the young, those who command her with audacity.

## Historical Examples to Reference

### Successful Princes
- **Cesare Borgia**: Master of the fox and lion, though undone by fortune
- **Francesco Sforza**: Rose by ability, kept power with little trouble
- **Ferdinand of Aragon**: Rose from insignificance through great enterprises
- **Pope Julius II**: Bold and successful in war

### Cautionary Examples
- **Louis XII of France**: Made five errors in Italy, lost everything
- **Agathocles of Syracuse**: Gained power through wickedness, but no glory
- **Oliverotto da Fermo**: Rose by treachery, destroyed by Borgia

### Ancient Models
- **Moses, Cyrus, Romulus, Theseus**: Armed prophets who conquered
- **Alexander the Great**: Held Asia because he destroyed the old order
- **Romans in Greece**: Masterful management of conquered territories

## Memorable Quotations

1. "Men ought either to be well treated or crushed, because they can avenge themselves of lighter injuries, of more serious ones they cannot."

2. "There is nothing more difficult to take in hand, more perilous to conduct, or more uncertain in its success, than to take the lead in the introduction of a new order of things."

3. "All armed prophets have conquered, and the unarmed ones have been destroyed."

4. "The wish to acquire is in truth very natural and common, and men always do so when they can."

5. "He who is the cause of another becoming powerful is ruined."

6. "Never let any Government imagine that it can choose perfectly safe courses; rather let it expect to have to take very doubtful ones."

7. "Princes ought to leave affairs of reproach to the management of others, and keep those of grace in their own hands."

8. "The best possible fortress is — not to be hated by the people."
